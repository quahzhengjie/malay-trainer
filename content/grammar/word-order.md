Malay sentences follow **subject – verb – object**, the same order as English:

> **Saya** (I) **makan** (eat) **nasi** (rice).

## Verbs never change for tense

This is the easiest thing about Malay grammar: a verb has **one form**. `makan`
is "eat", "eats", "ate" and "eaten" all at once. You show *when* something
happens with a separate word, never by changing the verb.

| Word | Meaning | Example |
|---|---|---|
| semalam | yesterday | Saya makan nasi semalam. |
| esok | tomorrow | Saya makan nasi esok. |
| sudah | already (done) | Saya sudah makan. |
| sedang | -ing (in progress) | Saya sedang makan. |
| akan | will | Saya akan makan. |
| belum | not yet | Saya belum makan. |

So you never conjugate — you add a time word or an aspect marker and the verb
stays exactly as it is.
